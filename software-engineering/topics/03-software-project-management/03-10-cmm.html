3.10 Capability Maturity Model (CMM) / SEI-CMM

← Topic 3

The Capability Maturity Model (CMM) is a framework developed by the Software Engineering Institute (SEI) at Carnegie Mellon University after studying organisations that contracted with the U.S. Department of Defence. CMM is a strategy for improving the software process so that an organisation can consistently produce quality software. It is used to judge the maturity of an organisation's software process and to identify the key practices needed to increase that maturity.

The term "maturity" refers to the degree of formality and optimisation of processes — from ad hoc practices, through formally defined steps, to managed metrics, and finally to active optimisation. A more mature process is more predictable in terms of time, cost, and quality.

Five maturity levels

CMM defines five sequential maturity levels. An organisation must stabilise each level before advancing to the next.

Level 1 — Initial

The process is unpredictable and poorly controlled. There is no real engineering management; everything is done on an ad hoc basis. Success depends heavily on the current staff — when people leave, the process leaves with them. Time and cost estimates are unreliable.

Level 2 — Repeatable

Basic project management practices are established. Planning and managing new projects are based on experience with similar previous projects. The organisation can repeat earlier successes on projects with comparable applications. Realistic plans are built on documented performance data from past work. Requirements management and software project tracking begin at this level.

Level 3 — Defined

The software process for developing and maintaining software is standardised and documented across the entire organisation, covering both engineering and management activities. Training programs ensure staff have the required skills. Risk management processes are integrated. Individual projects tailor the organisation's standard process to their needs rather than inventing their own from scratch.

Level 4 — Managed

The organisation sets quantitative quality goals for both products and processes. Process performance is measured with statistical techniques. The process becomes predictable with respect to time and cost because variation is understood and controlled.

Level 5 — Optimizing

The organisation analyses defects to determine their root causes and focuses on preventing defect occurrence. The company continuously improves process performance through incremental and innovative technological changes. This is the highest maturity level.

Key Process Areas (KPAs) at each level

Each maturity level is defined by specific Key Process Areas that an organisation must implement:

LevelKey Process Areas
1 — InitialNo formal KPAs — processes are ad hoc
2 — RepeatableProject planning, configuration management, requirements management, sub-contract management, software quality assurance
3 — DefinedPeer reviews, inter-group coordination, organisation process definition, organisation process focus, training programs
4 — ManagedSoftware quality management, quantitative process management
5 — OptimizingProcess change management, technology change management, defect prevention

CMM vs CMMI

CMM was later extended to CMMI (Capability Maturity Model Integration), which combines software engineering with systems engineering and other disciplines. For exam purposes, knowing the five CMM levels and their characteristics is usually sufficient. CMMI is also referenced in SQA topics (Topic 8).

Why CMM matters for SPM

CMM does not manage a single project — it improves the organisation's ability to manage all projects. A Level 1 organisation will struggle with estimation, risk management, and monitoring no matter how skilled the individual project manager is. Advancing to Level 2 and above institutionalises the SPM practices covered in this topic: planning, tracking, risk management, and quantitative control.

Exam question — CMM maturity levels

Q: What is the Capability Maturity Model? Describe the five maturity levels with key characteristics of each.

Model answer points:

  • CMM (SEI): framework to assess and improve software process maturity.
  • Maturity = degree of formality and optimisation of processes.
  • Level 1 Initial: ad hoc, unpredictable, no engineering management.
  • Level 2 Repeatable: basic PM, plans from past experience.
  • Level 3 Defined: standardised, documented process; training; risk management.
  • Level 4 Managed: quantitative goals; predictable time and cost.
  • Level 5 Optimizing: continuous improvement; defect root-cause analysis.
  • Levels are sequential — must stabilise each before advancing.
