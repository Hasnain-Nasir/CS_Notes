1.6 Software Crisis

← Topic 1

The software crisis refers to the set of serious problems faced by the software industry during the 1960s and 1970s (and still partially today). Old ad-hoc methods could not keep up with rapidly growing demand, complexity, and expectations. Projects suffered cost overruns, missed deadlines, poor quality, and failure to meet user requirements.

Edsger Dijkstra famously captured the scale of the problem:

"As long as there were no machines, programming was no problem at all; when we had a few weak computers, programming became a mild problem, and now we have gigantic computers, programming has become an equally gigantic problem." — Edsger Dijkstra, The Humble Programmer (EWD340)

Symptoms and causes

  • Many projects ran overtime, and the average project overshot its schedule by about half.
  • Software was often delivered late, over budget, or not delivered at all.
  • Delivered software had low quality because it contained many bugs and defects at release.
  • The cost of owning and maintaining software became as expensive as developing it in the first place.
  • Software frequently failed to meet the actual needs and requirements of users.
  • As systems grew more complex, they became difficult to alter, debug, and enhance.

Major problems in software development

  • Inadequate requirements gathering: Requirements were often ambiguous or incomplete, and communication with stakeholders was poor.
  • Poor project management: Projects suffered from weak planning, poor monitoring, inadequate risk assessment, and unsuitable technology choices.
  • Insufficient time and budget: Deadlines were unrealistic and resources were not allocated properly for the work required.
  • Lack of skilled personnel: Teams often lacked adequate expertise, and high staff turnover disrupted continuity.
  • Resistance to change: Organisations were reluctant to adopt new processes even as technology changed rapidly.

Contributing factors

  • Poor project management and a lack of adequate training in software engineering practices made large projects harder to control.
  • Teams often included less skilled members, and productivity did not improve enough to match growing demand.
  • There was a lack of standardisation, proper tools, and established methodologies in the early decades of computing.

Exploratory / build-and-fix style (what caused the crisis)

Before formal SE, many projects used an exploratory (build-and-fix) style: brief customer briefing → immediate coding → test and fix bugs in a loop until "good enough." No formal requirements analysis or process. This works only for tiny programs; for large systems it causes exponential growth in cost and unmaintainable code.

Solution direction

There is no single magic fix. The primary response was the emergence of Software Engineering as a systematic, disciplined, quantifiable approach — plus process models, standards, and better project management. Organisations were urged to control budgets, ensure quality, deliver on time, employ skilled people, and meet user requirements.

Exam question — Software Crisis

Q: What is the software crisis? Discuss its causes and how Software Engineering addresses it.

Model answer points:

  • The software crisis was the failure of ad-hoc methods in the 1960s and 1970s, leading to cost overruns, missed deadlines, poor quality, and failed deliveries.
  • Main causes included growing complexity, poor requirements gathering, weak project management, unskilled teams, and a build-and-fix development culture.
  • Including the Dijkstra quote can strengthen the answer and may earn extra marks in descriptive questions.
  • The solution was the rise of Software Engineering as a systematic discipline using defined processes, testing, standards, and a skilled workforce.
