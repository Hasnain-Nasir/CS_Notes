5.12 Design Strategies

← Topic 5

A design strategy is the overall approach used to transform requirements into a complete system design. Different strategies suit different project sizes, complexity levels, and requirement stability. The two most fundamental strategies — top-down and bottom-up — are covered in detail in §5.13.

Major design strategies

1. Top-down design

  • The problem is divided into smaller programs/modules according to user requirements; the process repeats until each sub-problem is simple enough to solve directly.
  • Also called stepwise refinement — start with a high-level description and refine each part in greater detail.
  • Best for small and medium-sized products with well-understood requirements.
  • Most commonly used strategy; associated with function-oriented design (§5.14).

2. Bottom-up design

  • Instead of understanding the entire problem first, solve sub-problems and keep combining solutions until the entire problem is solved.
  • Individual components are defined first, then integrated into larger modules.
  • Best for large, complex projects where the full problem is difficult to grasp as a whole.
  • Associated with object-oriented design and OOP languages (§5.15).

3. Iterative design

  • The system is designed and implemented in stages, with each stage building on the results of the previous stage.
  • Design is refined through repeated cycles of design → prototype → feedback → redesign.
  • Suitable when requirements evolve during development.

4. Incremental design

  • A small part of the system is designed and implemented at a time; more functionality is added with each iteration.
  • Each increment delivers usable functionality before the full system is complete.
  • Links to the Incremental Process Model in §2.8.

5. Agile design

  • A flexible, iterative approach where requirements and design evolve through collaboration between self-organising, cross-functional teams.
  • Design emerges through short sprints rather than a single upfront design phase.
  • Links to Agile in §2.11.

Comparison of top-down vs bottom-up (summary)

AspectTop-DownBottom-Up
Approach Decompose whole system into sub-problems top to bottom. Build and integrate components from bottom up.
Project size Small to medium products. Large, complex projects.
Architecture Systematic; easy to understand; modular. Complex architecture; harder to understand and manage.
Cost/time prediction Possible in correspondence with user requirements. Harder to predict upfront.
Design paradigm Function-oriented design (structured analysis). Object-oriented design (OOP languages).
Main disadvantage Cannot understand very large/complex problems as a whole. Complex architecture; difficult to manage.

Choosing a design strategy

  • Use top-down when requirements are well-defined, the system is small/medium, and a clear overall structure is needed upfront.
  • Use bottom-up when the system is large, components are reusable, and OOP is the implementation paradigm.
  • Use iterative/incremental when requirements may change or early delivery of partial functionality is needed.
  • Use agile design when the team needs maximum flexibility and continuous customer feedback.
Exam question — Design strategies

Q: Name and explain design strategies. When would you use top-down vs bottom-up?

Model answer points:

  • Name: top-down, bottom-up, iterative, incremental, agile.
  • Top-down: stepwise refinement; small/medium; FOD; systematic, predictable.
  • Bottom-up: build components first; large projects; OOD; reusable modules.
  • Iterative/incremental: evolving requirements; partial delivery.
