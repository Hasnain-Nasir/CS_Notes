4.1 Requirements Engineering — Overview

← Topic 4

Requirements Engineering (RE) is the systematic process of discovering, documenting, analysing, validating, and managing what a software system must do and the constraints under which it must operate. It is the bridge between the customer's problem domain and the developer's solution domain, and it is the first major technical phase of the SDLC after project initiation.

A requirement is a condition or capability that a system must satisfy — either because a user needs it to solve a problem, or because an external standard, contract, or regulation demands it. IEEE 729 defines a requirement as a condition or capability needed by a user to solve a problem or achieve an objective, or a condition or capability that must be met or possessed by a system to satisfy a contract, standard, specification, or other formally imposed document.

Why requirements engineering matters

Poor requirements are one of the most common causes of software project failure. RE aims to prevent:

  • Scope creep: Uncontrolled growth of features because the original scope was never clearly defined or agreed.
  • Wrong product: The team builds software that works correctly but does not solve the user's real problem.
  • Cost and schedule overrun: Late discovery of missing or incorrect requirements forces expensive rework during design, coding, or testing.
  • Customer dissatisfaction: Stakeholders reject the delivered system because their needs were never captured or validated.

Requirements engineering connects directly to Software Project Management: clear requirements enable accurate estimation, realistic scheduling, and effective change control.

Core RE activities at a glance

The RE process (detailed in §4.2) typically includes:

  • Feasibility study: Determine whether the proposed system is technically, economically, and operationally viable before committing resources.
  • Requirements elicitation: Discover and gather requirements from stakeholders through interviews, workshops, observation, and other techniques.
  • Requirements analysis: Structure, clarify, prioritise, and resolve conflicts among the gathered needs.
  • Requirements specification: Document requirements in a formal Software Requirements Specification (SRS) document.
  • Requirements validation (V&V): Review, prototype, and test requirements to confirm they are correct, complete, and feasible.
  • Requirements management: Track changes, maintain traceability, and keep requirements aligned with the evolving project.

Requirements vs design

Requirements describe what the system must do and the constraints it must satisfy; design describes how those requirements will be implemented. A good SRS stays at the requirements level — it should not prescribe internal data structures, algorithms, or UI layout unless those are genuine constraints imposed by the customer or environment.

Key stakeholders in RE

  • End users: People who will operate the system daily and whose tasks the software must support.
  • Customers / clients: Those who pay for the system and define business objectives.
  • Domain experts: Specialists who understand the business rules, regulations, and workflows the system must reflect.
  • Developers and testers: The team that must implement and verify the requirements.
  • Project managers: Responsible for scope, schedule, and change control based on the agreed requirements baseline.
Exam question — Define Requirements Engineering

Q: What is Requirements Engineering? Why is it critical in the SDLC?

Model answer points:

  • Define RE as the systematic process of discovering, documenting, analysing, validating, and managing system requirements.
  • Give IEEE 729 definition of a requirement (condition/capability needed by user or imposed by contract/standard).
  • List core activities: feasibility, elicitation, analysis, specification, validation, management.
  • Explain consequences of poor RE: scope creep, wrong product, cost overrun, customer rejection.
  • Distinguish requirements (what) from design (how); link to SDLC and SPM.
