4.16 Quality Characteristics of a Good SRS

← Topic 4

A good SRS is judged not only by its content but by how well it satisfies a set of quality characteristics. These characteristics guide both writing and review, and they map closely to the principles in IEEE 830 and IEEE 29148 (see §4.17).

Twelve quality characteristics

  • Correctness: Every requirement accurately reflects a genuine user need or constraint. No false or outdated statements.
  • Completeness: All significant functional and non-functional requirements are included. Nothing important is missing.
  • Consistency: Requirements do not contradict each other internally or with referenced models (DFD, ERD).
  • Unambiguous: Each requirement has only one interpretation. Vague adjectives are replaced with precise criteria.
  • Ranking: Requirements are prioritised (essential, conditional, optional) so the team knows what to defer under pressure.
  • Modifiable: The document structure allows changes without corrupting unrelated sections — unique IDs and modular sections help.
  • Verifiable: Each requirement can be checked by inspection, analysis, demonstration, or test. If you cannot verify it, rewrite it.
  • Traceable: Each requirement can be traced to its source (stakeholder, regulation) and forward to design and test artefacts.
  • Design independence: The SRS states what is needed, not how to build it, preserving freedom for designers.
  • Testable: Test cases can be derived directly from each requirement without further interpretation.
  • Understandable: Stakeholders, developers, and testers can read and comprehend the document without specialised knowledge beyond the glossary.
  • Right abstraction level: Requirements are neither too high-level (useless for design) nor too low-level (design disguised as requirements).

Using quality characteristics in review

During a formal SRS review, each section is checked against this checklist:

  • Are all functional requirements correct and complete?
  • Do any two requirements conflict (consistency)?
  • Can a tester write a test case for every requirement (testable/verifiable)?
  • Does every requirement have an ID and a trace link (traceable)?
  • Are priorities assigned (ranking)?
Exam question — SRS quality characteristics

Q: List and explain the quality characteristics of a good SRS.

Model answer points:

  • Name characteristics: correctness, completeness, consistency, unambiguous, ranking, modifiable, verifiable, traceable, design independence, testable, understandable, right abstraction level.
  • Give one-sentence explanation of each.
  • Explain how verifiable/testable link to acceptance testing.
  • Explain traceability forward and backward.
  • Describe use of checklist in formal SRS review.
