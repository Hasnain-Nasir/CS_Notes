4.15 How to Write a Good SRS

← Topic 4

Writing a good SRS requires discipline, stakeholder involvement, and adherence to quality guidelines. A poorly written SRS causes the same failures as poor requirements — misunderstandings, rework, and rejected deliverables. This section provides practical guidance; quality characteristics are detailed in §4.16 and IEEE structure in §4.17.

Before writing

  • Complete elicitation and analysis so the SRS reflects validated, prioritised requirements (see §4.3, §4.5).
  • Agree on the document template and standard (e.g. IEEE 830 / 29148) with all stakeholders.
  • Establish a glossary of domain terms before drafting detailed requirements.
  • Identify the audience for each section so the level of detail is appropriate.

Writing guidelines

  • One requirement per statement: Avoid compound requirements that combine multiple needs in one sentence; split them so each is independently testable.
  • Use active voice: "The system shall generate a report" is clearer than "A report shall be generated."
  • Be specific and measurable: Replace vague terms ("fast," "user-friendly") with quantifiable criteria ("response time ≤ 2 seconds").
  • Avoid design details: State what is required, not how to implement it, unless a design constraint is genuinely imposed.
  • Assign unique identifiers: Every requirement gets an ID (e.g. REQ-F-001) for traceability.
  • Use consistent terminology: Refer to the glossary; do not use synonyms for the same concept.
  • Include rationale where helpful: Brief notes on why a requirement exists aid review and future maintenance.
  • Cross-reference models: Link functional requirements to DFD processes and ERD entities where applicable.

Review and iteration

  • Draft the SRS iteratively — produce an outline first, then fill sections in priority order.
  • Conduct formal reviews with customers, developers, and testers before declaring the baseline (see §4.7).
  • Use a requirements quality checklist covering correctness, completeness, consistency, and testability.
  • Prototype ambiguous areas before finalising the corresponding SRS sections.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Gold-plating — adding features stakeholders did not request.
  • Copying from a previous project's SRS without adapting to the current context.
  • Leaving TBD ("to be determined") placeholders in the approved baseline.
  • Mixing requirements with project plan details (team structure, coding standards) that belong elsewhere.
Exam question — Writing a good SRS

Q: What guidelines should be followed when writing an SRS document?

Model answer points:

  • Mention preparation: complete elicitation/analysis, agree template, glossary, audience.
  • State writing rules: one req per statement, active voice, measurable, no design detail, unique ID, consistent terms.
  • Describe iterative drafting and formal review with stakeholders.
  • List common mistakes: gold-plating, copy-paste, TBD in baseline, mixing plan with requirements.
  • Cross-reference quality characteristics and IEEE standard.
