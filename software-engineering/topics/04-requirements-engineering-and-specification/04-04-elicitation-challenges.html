4.4 Challenges in Eliciting Requirements

← Topic 4

Requirements elicitation is inherently difficult because stakeholders, analysts, and developers each bring different perspectives, vocabularies, and levels of knowledge. Understanding these challenges helps analysts select appropriate techniques and manage expectations.

Stakeholder-related challenges

  • Stakeholders do not know what they want: Users often cannot articulate needs until they see a working system or prototype.
  • Conflicting requirements: Different stakeholders want incompatible features or priorities, requiring negotiation and trade-offs.
  • Changing requirements: Business conditions evolve during elicitation, making the requirements baseline unstable.
  • Stakeholder unavailability: Key domain experts may have limited time, delaying or limiting elicitation sessions.
  • Political and organisational barriers: Power dynamics, departmental silos, or fear of job displacement can suppress honest input.

Analyst and communication challenges

  • Language and terminology gaps: Users speak in business terms; developers speak in technical terms; misunderstandings are common.
  • Implicit assumptions: Stakeholders assume "obvious" requirements need not be stated, but developers lack that domain knowledge.
  • Ambiguity and vagueness: Words like "fast," "user-friendly," or "secure" mean different things to different people.
  • Analyst bias: The analyst may unconsciously impose a preferred solution rather than capturing the true need.

Domain and system challenges

  • Complex or unfamiliar domains: Safety-critical, financial, or scientific systems require deep domain expertise that the development team may lack.
  • Legacy system constraints: Existing data formats, interfaces, and business rules constrain the new system but may be poorly documented.
  • Regulatory requirements: Legal and compliance rules must be discovered from external documents, not from user interviews alone.
  • Scale: Large systems with many stakeholder groups make complete elicitation impractical without prioritisation.

Strategies to overcome elicitation challenges

  • Use multiple elicitation techniques to cross-check and fill gaps (see §4.3).
  • Build prototypes early so stakeholders can react to something concrete.
  • Establish a glossary of domain terms shared by all parties.
  • Prioritise stakeholders and requirements using MoSCoW or similar ranking (see §4.5).
  • Apply formal change control once a baseline is agreed (see §4.7 and SPM change management in Topic 3).
Exam question — Elicitation challenges

Q: What are the main challenges in eliciting software requirements? How can they be addressed?

Model answer points:

  • List stakeholder challenges: unknown needs, conflicts, changing requirements, unavailability.
  • List communication challenges: terminology gaps, implicit assumptions, ambiguity.
  • List domain challenges: complexity, legacy constraints, regulations, scale.
  • Propose strategies: multiple techniques, prototyping, glossary, prioritisation, change control.
  • Link to elicitation techniques and requirements management.
