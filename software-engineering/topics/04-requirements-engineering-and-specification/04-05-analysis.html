4.5 Analysis

← Topic 4

Requirements analysis is the process of structuring, refining, and evaluating the raw requirements gathered during elicitation. Its goal is to produce a consistent, complete, unambiguous, and prioritised set of requirements ready for specification and validation.

Objectives of requirements analysis

  • Detect and resolve conflicts: Identify contradictory requirements from different stakeholders and negotiate a resolution.
  • Identify gaps and redundancies: Find missing requirements and remove duplicate or overlapping statements.
  • Clarify ambiguity: Replace vague terms with measurable, testable statements.
  • Prioritise requirements: Rank requirements by business value, risk, or dependency so the team knows what to build first.
  • Classify requirements: Separate functional from non-functional, user from system, and domain-specific needs (see §4.9).
  • Model the system: Create DFDs, ERDs, and use-case diagrams to represent requirements visually (see §4.10).

Analysis techniques

  • Requirements categorisation: Group requirements by feature, subsystem, or stakeholder to reveal structure.
  • Conflict analysis: Compare requirements pairwise or in workshops to expose contradictions.
  • Prioritisation methods: MoSCoW (Must/Should/Could/Won't), ranking, or weighted scoring assign relative importance.
  • Decision tables: Tabulate complex conditional logic for unambiguous analysis (see §4.13).
  • Modelling: DFDs show data movement; ERDs show data structure; both expose inconsistencies in the written requirements.
  • Prototyping: A throwaway or evolutionary prototype reveals gaps when stakeholders interact with it.

Outputs of analysis

  • A refined, prioritised requirements list with conflicts resolved.
  • System models (DFD, ERD, use-case diagrams) consistent with the written requirements.
  • A requirements traceability matrix linking needs to sources and later design elements.
  • Identified risks and assumptions documented for the project manager (link to Topic 3 §3.8).

Common analysis pitfalls

  • Skipping analysis and moving directly to coding, discovering conflicts late in development.
  • Analysis paralysis — spending too long refining requirements without reaching a baseline.
  • Allowing design decisions to creep into requirements during analysis workshops.
Exam question — Requirements analysis

Q: What is requirements analysis? What are its objectives and main techniques?

Model answer points:

  • Define analysis as structuring, refining, and evaluating elicited requirements.
  • List objectives: resolve conflicts, find gaps, clarify ambiguity, prioritise, classify, model.
  • Describe techniques: categorisation, conflict analysis, MoSCoW, decision tables, modelling, prototyping.
  • Name outputs: prioritised list, models, traceability matrix, risks/assumptions.
  • Mention pitfalls: skipping analysis, analysis paralysis, design creep.
