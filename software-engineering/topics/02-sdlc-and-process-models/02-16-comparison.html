2.16 Comparison of Life Cycle Models

← Topic 2

No single SDLC model fits all projects. Examiners often ask for a master comparison and selection criteria. Use the table below as a revision anchor.

Master comparison table

ModelNatureRequirementsRisk handlingDeliveryBest suited for
WaterfallIt follows a strictly sequential process.Requirements are fixed upfront.Risks are often discovered late.The customer receives the product at the end of the project.It suits stable specifications and small or simple projects.
Iterative WaterfallIt is sequential but allows controlled feedback between phases.Requirements are mostly fixed, with limited revision.Some risk is reduced through backtracking when errors are found.Delivery still occurs mainly at the end of the project.It suits projects with moderate requirement stability.
PrototypeIt is iterative and centred on building prototypes.Requirements are unclear at first and are clarified through prototyping.Early user feedback reduces requirements risk.A prototype is delivered first, then the final product.It suits novel user interfaces and vague initial requirements.
SpiralIt is iterative and explicitly risk-driven.Requirements evolve across cycles.Risks are analysed and addressed in every cycle.Deliverables are produced at the end of each spiral cycle.It suits large, complex, high-risk projects.
IncrementalIt uses multiple mini-Waterfall cycles.Requirements are partitioned into increments.Partial early delivery reduces overall delivery risk.Each increment is delivered separately to the customer.It suits projects with prioritised features that can be released in parts.
RADIt is time-boxed and tool-driven.Requirements evolve during rapid development.User workshops help surface risks early.Working software is delivered in fast iterations.It suits small or medium projects with tight schedules.
Agile/XPIt uses short iterative sprints.Requirements are highly changeable.Continuous customer feedback manages risk.Working software is delivered every sprint.It suits dynamic requirements and fast-changing markets.
V-ModelIt is sequential with paired test phases.Requirements are fixed upfront.Test planning begins early, which improves risk control.Delivery occurs at the end of the project.It suits safety-critical and regulated systems.

Selection criteria (exam checklist)

  • Project size. Small projects often suit RAD or Agile, whereas large projects may need the Spiral model, Incremental delivery, or a structured Waterfall or V-Model approach.
  • Requirements stability. Stable requirements favour Waterfall or the V-Model, while volatile requirements favour Agile or the Prototype model.
  • Risk level. High-risk projects benefit from the Spiral model, whereas lower-risk projects can use Waterfall or Incremental approaches.
  • Customer availability. When customers can participate frequently, Agile or RAD is appropriate; when they cannot, Waterfall with a detailed SRS is more practical.
  • Time-to-market. Urgent delivery favours RAD, Agile, or Incremental models, while a flexible schedule allows Waterfall or the V-Model.
  • Regulatory and documentation needs. Strict audit or safety requirements favour the V-Model or Waterfall, whereas minimal documentation pressure allows a lighter Agile process.
Exam question — Model comparison and selection

Q: Compare major SDLC models in a table. What criteria should a project manager use to select a model?

Model answer points:

  • Draw the master comparison table and explain each model’s nature, requirements handling, risk handling, delivery pattern, and best use in full sentences.
  • Explain selection criteria such as project size, requirements stability, risk level, customer involvement, schedule pressure, and documentation needs.
  • Support the answer with scenario examples, such as a hospital system (V-Model), a startup app (Agile), or an ERP module delivered in increments.
  • Conclude that no single model is best for every project because the right choice depends on context.
